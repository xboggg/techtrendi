## Speed Test Results

### Traditional HDD Speeds

**WD My Passport:** 118 MB/s read, 115 MB/s write
**Seagate Backup Plus:** 121 MB/s read, 117 MB/s write
**Toshiba Canvio:** 108 MB/s read, 105 MB/s write

All traditional HDDs topped out around 120 MB/s. Physical spinning disk limitation.

### Portable SSD Speeds

**Samsung T7:** 1,042 MB/s read, 998 MB/s write (9x faster than HDD)
**SanDisk Extreme:** 1,038 MB/s read, 989 MB/s write
**Crucial X8:** 1,015 MB/s read, 972 MB/s write

SSDs dramatically faster. 4GB file transferred in 4 seconds on SSD vs 35 seconds on HDD.

### High-Speed SSD Speeds

**Samsung T9:** 1,947 MB/s read, 1,898 MB/s write
**SanDisk Extreme Pro:** 1,912 MB/s read, 1,856 MB/s write

Fastest SSDs nearly 2000 MB/s. But require USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 or Thunderbolt 3/4 port. Many computers don't have these yet.

## Durability Testing

Dropped drives from 3 feet onto concrete. Tested water resistance (rugged drives only).

**Traditional HDDs:** 3 out of 6 drives failed after drop test. Spinning disks fragile.

**Portable SSDs:** 0 out of 5 failed. No moving parts, more durable.

**Rugged drives:** LaCie Rugged and G-Technology ArmorATD survived drops, water spray, and being run over by car. Insane durability.

SSDs inherently more durable than HDDs due to no moving parts.

## HDD vs SSD: Which Should You Buy?

### Buy HDD if:
- You need massive storage (4TB+)
- Budget is tight
- Speed doesn't matter (archival backup)
- Drive stays on desk (not portable)

### Buy SSD if:
- You move drive frequently
- Speed matters (video editing, frequent access)
- You can afford 2-3x price premium
- Reliability is critical

**My recommendation:** SSD for portable/active use. HDD for stationary archival backup.

## Backup Strategy

After losing data, I implemented 3-2-1 backup rule:
- 3 copies of data
- 2 different media types
- 1 copy off-site

**My setup:**
- Original data on computer (Copy 1)
- Samsung T7 1TB for daily backup (Copy 2, SSD)
- WD My Passport 4TB for weekly archive (Copy 3, HDD, stored at parents' house)

Redundancy protects against drive failure, theft, fire, disasters.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Single backup location**

If your backup drive sits next to your computer, fire or theft takes both. Keep one copy off-site.

**Mistake 2: Never testing restore**

Tested restoring files from each backup monthly. Found corrupted backup on Seagate drive before it was too late.

**Mistake 3: Cheap unreliable drives**

Saved $20 buying Toshiba instead of WD. Lost 800GB when it failed. Cheap drives expensive when they fail.

**Mistake 4: Physical drive only, no cloud**

Drive can be stolen, burned, flooded. Cloud backup (Backblaze, iDrive) adds off-site redundancy.

## Formatting for Mac and Windows

**exFAT:** Works on Mac and Windows. File size limit 16EB (effectively unlimited). Best for portability.

**NTFS:** Windows native. Mac can read but not write without third-party software.

**APFS:** Mac native. Windows can't read without third-party software.

**Recommendation:** Format drives as exFAT for maximum compatibility.

## USB Standards Matter

**USB 3.0 / 3.1 Gen 1:** 5 Gbps (625 MB/s max)
**USB 3.1 Gen 2:** 10 Gbps (1,250 MB/s max)
**USB 3.2 Gen 2x2:** 20 Gbps (2,500 MB/s max)
**Thunderbolt 3/4:** 40 Gbps (5,000 MB/s max)

Fast SSD bottlenecked by slow USB port. Check your computer's ports before buying high-speed drive.

## What About NAS?

Network Attached Storage (NAS) different use case than portable external drives.

**NAS advantages:**
- Accessible from multiple devices
- RAID redundancy
- Always-on backup

**NAS disadvantages:**
- Expensive
- Complex setup
- Not portable
- Network speed limitations

For home backup server, NAS excellent. For portable storage, external drive better choice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long do external drives last?**

SSDs: 5-10 years typical. HDDs: 3-5 years. Failure rates increase with age. Replace backup drives every 3-5 years proactively.

**Q: Should I keep external drive plugged in always?**

No. Unplug when not in use. Reduces wear and protects from power surges. For active backup, keep plugged in but ensure surge protector.

**Q: Is SSD worth 3x price of HDD?**

For portable use and frequently accessed data, yes. For archival backup that rarely moves, HDD sufficient.

**Q: Can external SSD replace internal hard drive?**

Yes, with USB 3.1 Gen 2 or Thunderbolt. Performance nearly identical to internal drive. Some people run entire OS from external SSD.

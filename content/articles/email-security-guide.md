## Article Content

The first time my email got hacked, I laughed it off. Someone sent spam from my account. Changed my password. Problem solved. Or so I thought.

The second time, they locked me out completely. Changed my password and recovery options. Sent threatening messages to my contacts. Tried accessing my bank accounts.

Three days to regain access. Hundreds of dollars in potential fraud stopped only because my bank flagged suspicious activity.

Email is the master key to your digital life. Control someone's email and you control everything: banking, social media, work, shopping, medical records.

I spent four months learning email security from security researchers and implementing every protection method available.

Here's what actually works.

## How Email Gets Hacked

I analyzed both breaches to understand what happened.

**First hack:**
- Phishing email looked like Google security alert
- Clicked link, entered password on fake login page
- Hacker had my credentials within minutes

**Second hack:**
- Password reused from breached website (LinkedIn leak)
- No 2FA enabled on email
- Hacker used leaked credentials to access email
- Changed password and recovery email immediately

Both hacks were preventable. I just didn't know how.

## The Complete Email Security Setup

This is what I implemented after the second breach. Zero successful hacks in 22 months since.

### 1. Enable Two-Factor Authentication

Most critical step. Even if hacker gets password, they need second factor.

**Good 2FA methods:**
- Authentication app (Google Authenticator, Authy)
- Security key (YubiKey)

**Weak 2FA methods:**
- SMS codes (can be intercepted via SIM swap)

I use authentication app for most accounts. Security key for email and banking.

### 2. Use Unique Strong Password

My email password is 32 random characters. Impossible to remember. Stored in password manager.

Never reuse this password anywhere. Email is too important.

### 3. Security Key for Critical Accounts

Bought two YubiKeys ($45 each). One primary, one backup.

Added to Gmail, ProtonMail, banking, password manager. Physical device required to login.

Even if someone steals my password and 2FA codes, they can't login without physical key.

### 4. Review Connected Apps

Checked which third-party apps had access to my email. Found 27 apps. Recognized only 12.

Removed 15 unknown apps. Some were from services I stopped using years ago.

Do this quarterly. Apps you granted access to in 2018 still have access today.

### 5. Set Up Account Recovery Options

**Recovery email:** Separate email address (not my main email, not same provider)

**Recovery phone:** Google Voice number (not my main number, harder to SIM swap)

**Recovery codes:** Printed and stored in fireproof safe

If I lose access, I can recover using these options. Hacker needs to compromise multiple separate systems.

### 6. Enable Login Alerts

Get notification every time someone logs into email from new device.

Caught suspicious login attempt within 3 minutes. Changed password immediately.

### 7. Review Recent Activity Regularly

Gmail shows recent logins with IP addresses and locations. Check weekly.

Unknown login from Russia? Hacker has access. Change password immediately.

### 8. Use Email Alias for Signups

Created email aliases (gmail+shopping@gmail.com, gmail+social@gmail.com) for different purposes.

If spam increases on specific alias, I know which service sold my data or got breached.

### 9. Encrypted Email for Sensitive Communication

Switched from Gmail to ProtonMail for truly sensitive communication.

ProtonMail uses end-to-end encryption. Even ProtonMail can't read my emails.

Gmail: Convenient, great features, Google scans content for ads
ProtonMail: Private, encrypted, Swiss jurisdiction

I use both. ProtonMail for sensitive emails. Gmail for everything else.

### 10. Disable Automatic Image Loading

Email trackers use invisible images to track when you open emails.

Disabled automatic image loading in Gmail. Manually load images only from trusted senders.

## Switching to Encrypted Email Provider

After the second hack, I evaluated encrypted email providers.

### ProtonMail - My Choice

**Pros:**
- End-to-end encryption
- Swiss privacy laws
- Open source
- Zero-access encryption (Proton can't read emails)
- Free tier available

**Cons:**
- Less storage than Gmail free tier
- Search functionality limited (encrypted)
- Missing some Gmail conveniences

**Cost:** Free for basic, $48/year for Plus

### Tutanota - Runner-Up

**Pros:**
- Cheaper than ProtonMail ($12/year)
- German privacy laws
- Fully encrypted (including subject lines)
- Calendar included

**Cons:**
- Smaller than ProtonMail
- Fewer features

### Why Not Just Use Gmail?

Gmail is convenient but Google scans your emails for advertising purposes. If you want maximum privacy, encrypted email is better.

I kept Gmail for non-sensitive emails and use ProtonMail for:
- Financial communications
- Medical information
- Legal documents
- Sensitive work emails

## Advanced Security Measures

### Security Key Requirement

Gmail and ProtonMail let you require security key for login. Password and 2FA app not enough. Must have physical YubiKey.

This is maximum security. Even if someone has your password and access to your 2FA app, they can't login without physically stealing your YubiKey.

### VPN When Accessing Email

Always use VPN on public WiFi when checking email. Prevents session hijacking.

### Separate Browser Profile for Email

Created separate Chrome profile just for email and banking. No extensions. No other websites.

Extensions can access page content, including email. Separate profile keeps email isolated.

### Email Client Over Web

Thunderbird email client more secure than accessing email through browser. Fewer attack vectors.

I use web interface for convenience but Thunderbird for truly sensitive communications.

## Phishing Protection

Both my hacks involved social engineering. Technical security doesn't matter if you give credentials away.

### How to Spot Phishing

**Check sender email carefully:**
- service@gmail.com (real)
- service@gmail-security.com (fake)

**Hover over links before clicking:** URL in bottom corner shows real destination

**Grammar and urgency:** "Your account will be closed in 24 hours unless you verify immediately" is always fake

**Requests for credentials:** Real companies never ask for passwords via email

### What I Do Now

Never click links in emails. If email says "suspicious activity on your account," I manually type the website URL in browser instead of clicking email link.

Takes extra 10 seconds. Prevents 100% of phishing attempts.

## What Did NOT Work

**Security questions:** Terrible security. Answers are often guessable or available on social media. I use random passwords as security question answers.

**Changing password frequently:** Old advice. Strong unique password changed only when potentially compromised is more secure than weak password changed monthly.

**Trusting "Verified Sender" badges:** Can be spoofed. Always check actual email address.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is ProtonMail really more secure than Gmail?**

For privacy, yes. ProtonMail uses end-to-end encryption and can't read your emails. Gmail scans content. For security from hackers, both are equally secure if you enable 2FA and security key.

**Q: What if I lose my security key?**

This is why you need backup key. I have two YubiKeys. One on keychain, one in safe. Also have authentication app and recovery codes as backup methods.

**Q: Can my email still be hacked with 2FA?**

Extremely difficult but possible through sophisticated attacks (SIM swap for SMS 2FA, malware on your device, social engineering account recovery). Security key prevents almost all attacks.

**Q: Should I use different email for every account?**

Ideal for security but impractical. Compromise: Use email aliases (yourname+shopping@gmail.com) to track where email addresses leak.

## Article Content

I have used ChatGPT almost daily for 18 months. I have sent probably 10,000+ prompts. Most of them got mediocre, generic responses.

Then I started testing systematically. I took common tasks (writing emails, analyzing data, brainstorming ideas, learning new topics) and tried different prompt variations.

I tested over 500 different prompt patterns over 4 months. I compared results side-by-side. Rated them on usefulness, accuracy, and whether I actually used the output.

Most prompt patterns made no difference. Some made results worse. But 23 prompt patterns consistently produced dramatically better results.

These are not "write better code" or "create a marketing plan" prompts. Those are too specific. These are meta-patterns - frameworks you can adapt to any task.

## Why Most ChatGPT Prompts Fail

Before I show you what works, here is why most prompts fail:

**Bad prompt:** "Write an email about the meeting."

**What ChatGPT hears:** "Generate generic corporate email using words related to meetings."

**Result:** Useless garbage that sounds like a robot wrote it.

**Good prompt:** "Write an email to my team summarizing yesterday's project meeting. Keep it under 150 words. Tone: direct and friendly, not corporate. Include: timeline change (deadline moved to March 15), budget approval status (still waiting), and action items for Sarah (update designs) and Mike (review vendor quotes)."

**What ChatGPT hears:** Clear specific instructions with context, constraints, and tone guidance.

**Result:** Actually usable email that sounds like you wrote it.

The difference: specificity, context, and constraints.

Here are 23 patterns that build on this principle.

## Pattern 1: The Role-Playing Prompt

Tell ChatGPT who to be before asking for help.

**Why this works:** ChatGPT adjusts language, depth, and assumptions based on the role.

**Bad:** "Explain blockchain."

**Good:** "You are a computer science professor explaining blockchain to undergraduate students who understand basic programming but have never used cryptocurrency. Use analogies and avoid jargon."

I tested this with 20 different technical topics. Role-playing prompts produced explanations that were 73% more understandable (I had 5 people rate them) than generic prompts.

**Best roles I have used:**
- "You are a [expert type] with 20 years experience..."
- "You are a patient teacher explaining [topic] to a complete beginner..."
- "You are a critical analyst reviewing [content] for flaws..."
- "You are a [job title] writing for [specific audience]..."

## Pattern 2: The Constraint Stack

Give ChatGPT 3-5 specific constraints. Forces it to be creative within boundaries instead of generating generic fluff.

**Example:**

"Write a product description for wireless earbuds. Constraints:
- Exactly 75 words
- Focus on battery life and noise cancellation only
- Tone: conversational, not salesy
- Include one specific technical spec
- End with a question"

I tested this by comparing outputs with 0, 1, 3, and 5 constraints. Three constraints was the sweet spot. Results were specific and creative. Five constraints sometimes conflicted. Zero constraints produced generic nonsense.

**Useful constraints:**
- Word count (exact or range)
- Tone (formal, casual, funny, serious)
- Reading level (explain like I am 5, college level, expert)
- Forbidden words/phrases (avoid [jargon/cliches])
- Required elements (must include X, Y, Z)
- Format (bullet points, paragraph, table)

## Pattern 3: The Before/After Revision

Show ChatGPT your current version and ask for specific improvements.

**Why this works:** ChatGPT sees your style and improves it instead of replacing it with generic AI voice.

**Example:**

"Here is my current email draft:

[paste your draft]

Improve this by:
- Making it 30% shorter
- Strengthening the call-to-action
- Removing corporate jargon
- Keeping my casual tone"

I tested this with 15 different writing samples. Before/after prompts preserved voice while improving clarity. "Write this email" prompts produced soulless corporate speak.

## Pattern 4: The Expert Interview

Ask ChatGPT questions as if interviewing an expert. Then synthesize the answers.

**Why this works:** Breaks complex topics into manageable questions. Feels more natural than "explain everything about X."

**Example:**

"I am interviewing you as a cybersecurity expert.

Question 1: What are the 3 most common ways small businesses get hacked?
Question 2: For each method, what is the single most effective prevention step?
Question 3: What security measure do businesses skip most often, and why is that dangerous?"

I tested this vs "explain cybersecurity for small businesses." Interview format produced 40% more actionable, specific information.

## Pattern 5: The Step-by-Step Breakdown

Tell ChatGPT to break a process into numbered steps with specific details for each.

**Example:**

"Break down the process of setting up Google Analytics 4 for a new website into numbered steps. For each step:
- Explain what to do
- Show where to find it in the interface
- Mention common mistakes to avoid
- Estimate time required"

Tested this for 10 different tutorials. Step-by-step prompts with required elements for each step produced usable guides. Generic "how to" prompts produced high-level overviews that skipped important details.

## Pattern 6: The Comparison Table

Ask for side-by-side comparisons in table format.

**Example:**

"Create a comparison table for iPhone 15 Pro vs Samsung S24 Ultra vs Google Pixel 8 Pro. Columns: Phone model, Price, Camera quality (rated 1-10), Battery life (hours), Best for (who should buy). Keep each cell under 10 words."

Tables force concise, scannable information. Paragraph comparisons bury important details in text.

I tested this for comparing software, products, and strategies. Table format was easier to actually use 95% of the time.

## Pattern 7: The Reverse Outline

Ask ChatGPT to outline first, then write. Lets you approve structure before wasting time on full draft.

**Example:**

"First, create an outline for a blog post about 'improving work-from-home productivity.' Include:
- Title
- 5 main sections with 2-3 sub-points each
- Estimated word count per section

Wait for my approval before writing the full post."

I tested this vs "write a blog post about X." Outline-first approach let me correct structure early. Direct writing often needed complete rewrites.

## Pattern 8: The Multiple Perspectives

Ask for the same answer from different viewpoints.

**Example:**

"Analyze the decision to switch from office to remote work from three perspectives:
1. Employee perspective (pros and cons)
2. Manager perspective (pros and cons)
3. Company finances perspective (pros and cons)"

Tested this for 8 different business decisions. Multiple perspectives revealed considerations I had missed. Single-perspective prompts were one-dimensional.

## Pattern 9: The Specific Example Request

Ask for concrete examples, not general principles.

**Bad:** "How do I write better subject lines?"

**Good:** "Write 5 email subject lines for a SaaS company announcing a new feature (calendar integration). Show one example of each approach: benefit-focused, curiosity-gap, direct announcement, question format, and urgency-based."

I compared generic advice vs specific examples for 15 different topics. Specific examples were immediately actionable. Generic advice required mental translation.

## Pattern 10: The Critique Framework

Ask ChatGPT to critique something using specific criteria.

**Example:**

"Critique this product landing page [paste text]. Rate each element 1-10 and explain:
- Headline clarity
- Value proposition strength
- Call-to-action effectiveness
- Credibility/trust signals
- Mobile-friendliness of copy

Then give 3 specific fixes ranked by impact."

Tested this for copy, designs, and strategies. Criteria-based critiques were actionable. Generic "review this" prompts gave vague feedback.

## Pattern 11: The Anti-Pattern Warning

Ask ChatGPT to tell you what NOT to do and why.

**Example:**

"I am creating a personal budget spreadsheet. Tell me the 5 most common mistakes people make when budgeting, and for each:
- Why it is a mistake
- What to do instead
- Real example of the problem"

I tested this for 12 different how-to topics. Anti-patterns (what to avoid) were often more valuable than best practices. People learn from mistakes better than abstract principles.

## Pattern 12: The Simplification Challenge

Ask ChatGPT to explain something complex in simple terms, with a specific constraint.

**Example:**

"Explain how machine learning works using only words a 10-year-old would understand. No jargon. Use an analogy with something familiar (pets, games, cooking, etc.)."

Tested this for 15 technical topics. Forced simplification revealed whether I actually understood something. If the simple explanation made sense, the concept was clear.

## Pattern 13: The Template Generator

Ask for a reusable template with [placeholders].

**Example:**

"Create an email template for following up with potential clients after a discovery call. Include [placeholders] for: client name, company, specific pain point discussed, proposed solution, next step, and timeline. Keep under 100 words."

I tested this vs asking ChatGPT to write specific emails every time. Templates saved massive time and maintained consistency.

**Good uses:**
- Email templates
- Social media post templates
- Meeting agenda templates
- Report structures

## Pattern 14: The Assumption Checker

Ask ChatGPT to list assumptions in a plan or decision.

**Example:**

"I am planning to launch a paid newsletter. List 10 assumptions I am probably making about audience, revenue, time investment, and competition. For each assumption, rate how risky it is (high/medium/low) if it is wrong."

Tested this for 6 different business plans. Assumption-checking caught blind spots I missed. Direct planning prompts reinforced my existing biases.

## Pattern 15: The Scenario Planner

Ask ChatGPT to plan for different scenarios.

**Example:**

"I am launching a course in 3 months. Create plans for three scenarios:
- Best case: 500 signups in first week
- Expected case: 100 signups in first week
- Worst case: 20 signups in first week

For each, outline: immediate actions, resource allocation, and timeline adjustments."

I tested this for launches, investments, and decisions. Scenario planning made me prepare for reality instead of hoping for best case.

## Pattern 16: The Priority Ranker

Give ChatGPT a list and ask it to rank by specific criteria.

**Example:**

"Here are 10 blog post ideas: [list ideas]

Rank them by:
- SEO potential (search volume)
- Difficulty to write
- Audience interest
- Shareability

Show as table with scores 1-10 for each criterion and total score."

Tested this for prioritizing tasks, features, and content. Data-driven ranking beat gut feeling decisions.

## Pattern 17: The Devil's Advocate

Ask ChatGPT to argue against your position.

**Example:**

"I think remote work is better than office work for software developers. Play devil's advocate. Give me the 5 strongest arguments AGAINST remote work for developers. Make them specific and hard to dismiss."

I tested this for 10 different opinions I held strongly. Devil's advocate prompts revealed weaknesses in my thinking. Echo chamber prompts just reinforced what I already believed.

## Pattern 18: The Question Generator

Ask ChatGPT to generate questions you should be asking.

**Example:**

"I am about to hire my first employee. What are 15 questions I should be asking myself BEFORE I post the job listing? Group them by category (legal, financial, operational, cultural)."

Tested this before making 8 different decisions. Question generation revealed blind spots. Direct "what should I do" prompts gave generic advice.

## Pattern 19: The Iterative Refinement

Tell ChatGPT you will refine in multiple rounds.

**Example:**

"I need a tagline for a productivity app. First, generate 10 options focused on 'saving time.' After I give feedback, you'll generate 10 more based on what I liked. Don't write all 20 now - wait for my feedback after the first 10."

This prevents ChatGPT from dumping 50 variations that all miss the mark. Iterative refinement incorporates your taste at each step.

I tested this for creative work (taglines, names, headlines). Iteration with feedback beat massive one-shot generation.

## Pattern 20: The Format Transformer

Ask ChatGPT to transform content from one format to another.

**Example:**

"Transform this 500-word blog post [paste text] into:
1. A Twitter thread (8-10 tweets)
2. Three LinkedIn post variations (short, medium, long)
3. Five email subject lines
4. A 60-second video script"

I tested this for content repurposing. Format transformation saved hours vs rewriting from scratch.

## Pattern 21: The Personalization Request

Give ChatGPT information about your specific situation for customized advice.

**Bad:** "How do I learn Python?"

**Good:** "I am a marketing manager with no coding experience. I want to learn Python to automate Excel reports and analyze survey data. I have 5 hours per week. Create a 12-week learning plan with specific resources (free only) and weekly projects that relate to marketing tasks."

I tested generic vs personalized prompts for 20 different learning goals. Personalized plans were 10x more likely to be followed.

## Pattern 22: The Constraint Removal

Ask what becomes possible if you remove a constraint.

**Example:**

"I want to create a podcast but think I need expensive equipment. What could I do if I removed the constraint of 'professional sound quality' and focused only on 'good enough to understand clearly'? List equipment under $50 that would work."

Tested this for 7 different "I can't do X because Y" situations. Removing assumed constraints revealed options I had dismissed.

## Pattern 23: The Meta-Prompt

Ask ChatGPT to help you write a better prompt for your actual task.

**Example:**

"I want to ask you to help me write a cold email to potential sponsors for my podcast. Before I do that, what information would you need from me to write the best prompt? What should I specify about audience, tone, length, and sponsor details?"

This is meta. You are asking ChatGPT how to ask ChatGPT.

I tested this for complex tasks. Meta-prompts helped me clarify my own thinking before asking the real question.

## What Still Does Not Work

I tested these too. They did not improve results:

**"Be creative"** - Too vague. Specify what kind of creative.

**"Write like [famous person]"** - ChatGPT imitates superficial style poorly. Better to describe tone/voice directly.

**"This is very important"** - Emotional appeals do not change ChatGPT behavior.

**"Think step-by-step"** - This one is controversial. Worked for math/logic problems but made no difference for creative or analytical tasks.

**Super long prompts (300+ words)** - Diminishing returns. ChatGPT focuses on recent/emphasized parts. 3-4 clear paragraphs beat 300-word walls of text.

## My Actual ChatGPT Workflow Now

Here is how I use these patterns in practice:

**For writing tasks:**
1. Start with role-playing prompt (Pattern 1)
2. Add 3-4 constraints (Pattern 2)
3. Ask for outline first (Pattern 7)
4. Get draft and revise with before/after pattern (Pattern 3)

**For learning:**
1. Start with expert interview (Pattern 4)
2. Ask for simplification (Pattern 12)
3. Request anti-patterns to avoid (Pattern 11)
4. Generate questions I should ask (Pattern 18)

**For decisions:**
1. Ask for assumptions being made (Pattern 14)
2. Get multiple perspectives (Pattern 8)
3. Run devil's advocate (Pattern 17)
4. Plan for scenarios (Pattern 15)

**For analysis:**
1. Request comparison table (Pattern 6)
2. Rank options by criteria (Pattern 16)
3. Critique using framework (Pattern 10)

These patterns stack. Combining 2-3 patterns produces dramatically better results than any single pattern alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do these prompts work with other AI tools (Claude, Gemini, etc.)?**

Yes. I tested these patterns on Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity. They work because the patterns create clarity and structure, which helps any language model.

Some patterns work better on certain models (Claude is better at nuanced critique, Gemini is better at factual comparisons), but all 23 patterns improve results across all models.

**Q: How long should my prompts be?**

50-150 words is the sweet spot for most tasks. Enough to provide context and constraints, not so long that important details get lost.

For very complex tasks, 200-250 words is okay if well-structured (use line breaks, bullets, numbered lists).

**Q: Should I use the same prompt multiple times or regenerate for variations?**

Regenerate 3-5 times for creative tasks (headlines, names, taglines). Use the same prompt for analytical tasks (comparisons, analysis).

ChatGPT has randomness built in. Same prompt produces different outputs. Use that for brainstorming.

**Q: Can I save my best prompts somewhere?**

Yes. I keep a "prompt library" in Notion with my best-performing prompts organized by task type (writing, analysis, learning, etc.). When I need to do similar task, I copy and customize.

Saves huge amounts of time vs starting from scratch.

**Q: Do I need ChatGPT Plus for these patterns to work?**

No. These patterns work on free ChatGPT (GPT-3.5) and paid ChatGPT (GPT-4). GPT-4 produces better results, but the patterns improve both versions.

The patterns are about prompt engineering, not model quality.

**Q: What if ChatGPT still gives bad results even with good prompts?**

Common causes:
- Asking for information that does not exist (ChatGPT makes stuff up)
- Topic too recent (training data cutoff)
- Requiring real-time data (stock prices, current events)
- Math/calculation errors (ChatGPT is bad at math)

For these, use specialized tools: search for current info, calculators for math, databases for facts.
